Headline Detective

Before you see today's story, let's understand the trick you're about to spot.

What is headline bias?

  • Headlines are written to be read in under two seconds — accuracy often loses to attention.
  • Bias shows up through exaggeration, vague sourcing ("experts say"), loaded verbs, and missing context.
  • A biased headline can be technically true while still creating a false impression.

Why does it matter?

  • Most readers share headlines without reading the article — the headline becomes the "fact" they remember.
  • Repeated exposure to exaggerated framing shifts how urgent or extreme readers perceive reality to be.

How it affects everyday readers

  • It nudges emotional reactions (outrage, panic, excitement) before critical thinking kicks in.
  • Over time it can erode trust in real journalism, or conversely, build blind trust in sensational sources.

Emotion Detector

This one is about feeling, not just reading. Let's set up why that matters.

What is emotional manipulation?

  • Content engineered with specific words, framing, or pacing designed to trigger a fast emotional reaction.
  • Common levers: fear ("this could happen to you"), outrage ("they don't want you to know"), and curiosity gaps ("you won't believe...").

Why media uses it

  • Emotional content is shared 2-3x more than neutral content, driving reach, ad revenue, and engagement metrics.
  • Strong feelings shorten the time between seeing something and acting on it — sharing, commenting, buying.

Why recognizing it matters

  • Once you can name the technique, its emotional grip weakens — you can respond instead of react.
  • It protects your attention, your mood, and the accuracy of your worldview.
